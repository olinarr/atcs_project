'''Patrick Joseph "Pat" Toomey, Sr.''' (born November 17, 1961)<ref>[http://www.legistorm.com/memberbio/805/Rep_Pat_Toomey_PA.html Legistorm.com]</ref> is the [[Seniority in the United States Senate|junior]] [[United States Senate|United States Senator]] for [[Pennsylvania]]<ref>{{cite news| url=http://www.latimes.com/news/la-pn-sestak-toomey-final,0,7879406.story | work=Los Angeles Times | first=Michael A. | last=Memoli | date=November 2, 2010 |title=Pat Toomey holds off Joe Sestak for Senate seat in Pennsylvania}}</ref> and a member of the [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican Party]]. Previously, Toomey served as a [[United States House of Representatives|United States Representative]] for three terms, but he did not seek a fourth in compliance with a pledge he had made while running for office in 1998. <ref name=houseelect/> He also served as president of the [[Club for Growth]].  . 

Toomey attended school at [[La Salle Academy, Providence|La Salle Academy]] in [[Providence, Rhode Island]] before earning an [[A.B.]] in government from [[Harvard College]]. He was employed first by [[Chemical Bank]] and subsequently [[Morgan, Grenfell & Co.]] beginning in 1984 and 1986, respectively, until resigning from the latter in 1991. <ref name=ds/> . 

Prior to being elected to the U.S. Senate, Toomey served as the U.S. Representative for {{ushr|PA|15}} from 1999 to 2005. He narrowly lost the Republican primary for United States Senate in 2004. From 2005 to 2009, he served as president of the Club for Growth. <ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.clubforgrowth.org/aboutus|title=Club for Growth website|publisher=Clubforgrowth.org|date=|accessdate=2010-06-13}}</ref> After becoming the Republican nominee for the [[United States Senate election in Pennsylvania, 2010|2010 U.S. Senate election]] in Pennsylvania, Toomey was elected to the seat on November 2, 2010, defeating his  [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic]] opponent, retired [[Admiral (United States)|Admiral]] and [[Pennsylvania's 7th congressional district|Congressman]] [[Joe Sestak]].  . 

==Early life, education, and early career==. 
Born in [[Providence, Rhode Island]], Toomey was raised by [[Roman Catholic Church|Catholic]] parents, and was the third of six children. His father, Patrick, was a union worker who laid cable for Narragansett Electric Company, and his mother, Mary Ann, worked as a part-time secretary at St. Martha's, a Catholic church. <ref name=usnews>{{cite news|last=Kim|first=Mallie Jane|title=10 Things You Didn't Know About Pat Toomey|url=http://www.usnews.com/news/articles/2010/11/15/10-things-you-didnt-know-about-pat-toomey|accessdate=23 January 2012|newspaper=U.S.News & World Report|date=November 15, 2010}}</ref> . 

Toomey attended [[La Salle Academy, Providence|La Salle Academy]] on scholarship<ref>Keith Herbert Only change in 15th District: Stakes higher ** Toomey, O'Brien debating same issues as two years ago. 
[SECOND Edition] Morning Call - Allentown, Pa Oct 29, 2002 Page: B.1</ref> and graduated from [[Harvard College]] with an [[A.B.]] in government.  . 

Toomey was hired by [[Chemical Banking|Chemical Bank]] in 1984, where he was involved in [[currency swap]] transactions. In 1986, Toomey was hired by [[Morgan, Grenfell & Co.]], where he dealt in multiple foreign currencies, interest rates, and currency-related [[derivative (finance)|derivatives]]. <ref name=ds>{{cite news|title=Patrick Toomey: From Wall Street to Capitol Hill|first=Robert|last=Hunter|work=Derivatives Strategy|date=May 1999|url=http://www.derivativesstrategy.com/magazine/archive/1999/0599playf753.asp?print}}</ref> In 1991, Toomey resigned from the firm when it was acquired by [[Deutsche Bank]] in order to avoid the decreased flexibility and entrepreneurship that the acquisition would have caused. <ref name=ds/> The same year, Toomey and two younger brothers, Steven and Michael, opened Rookie's Restaurant in [[Allentown, Pennsylvania]]. <ref name=ds/> . 

In 1994, at 32, Toomey was elected to Allentown's newly established Government Study Commission. During his term, Toomey drafted a new charter for the Commission requiring a super-majority for any tax increase. <ref name="Time has come">{{Cite news|last=Micek|first=John L.|authorlink=John Micek|last2=Kraus|first2=Scott|last3=Isherwood|first3=Darryl R.|title=Pat Toomey's time has come|newspaper=The Morning Call|date=30 April 2009|url=http://www.mcall.com/news/local/all-a1_5toomey.6878251apr30,0,7770043.story}}</ref> The charter was approved by Allentown voters on April 23, 1996. <ref name=Charter>{{Cite book|editor-last=City of Allentown City Clerk’s Office|title=City of Allentown Home Rule Charter|year=2009|publisher=City of Allentown|url=http://www.allentownpa.gov/LinkClick.aspx?fileticket=1BxaqwA6Xzc%3D&tabid=67}}</ref> . 

==U.S. House of Representatives==. 
===Elections===. 
[[File:Pat Toomey Congress.jpg|thumb|125px|Congressman Toomey's Official Portrait.]] 
In 1998, Toomey ran for the 15th District seat being vacated by the Democratic incumbent [[Paul F. McHale, Jr.|Paul McHale]] against state Senator and future [[List of mayors of Allentown, Pennsylvania|Allentown Mayor]] [[Roy C. Afflerbach|Roy Afflerbach]]. <ref name=houseelect>{{cite news|last=Raju|first=Manu|title=Specter's future rests with Toomey|publisher=Politico.com|date=10 December 2008|url=http://www.politico.com/news/stories/1208/16387.html|accessdate=3 May 2009}}</ref> . 

During the campaign, Toomey criticized the agenda of the [[Presidency of Bill Clinton|Clinton-Gore administration]], specifically plans to modify the [[Internal Revenue Service]]. He criticized the plan by claiming that it did not "address the real fundamental problems plaguing American taxpayers", arguing that the IRS should be abolished. <ref name=1998irs>{{cite news|last=Frassinelli|first=Mike|title=Toomey Plan Says IRS Should Be Abolished|url=http://pqasb.pqarchiver.com/mcall/access/27496655.html?dids=27496655:27496655&FMT=ABS&FMTS=ABS:FT&type=current&date=Mar+19%2C+1998&author=MIKE+FRASSINELLI%2C+The+Morning+Call&pub=Morning+Call&desc=TOOMEY+PLAN+SAYS+IRS+SHOULD+BE+ABOLISHED+*+CANDIDATE+FOR+CONGRESS+SAYS+GORE'E+IDEAS+FALL+SHORT+OF+WHAT+TAXPAYERS+NEED.&pqatl=google|accessdate=16 August 2011|newspaper=The Morning Call|date=19 March 1998}}</ref> Later in the campaign, Toomey and Democratic opponent [[Roy C. Afflerbach]] debated the effectiveness of a [[flat tax]]-based system, an issue on which the two sharply disagreed. <ref name=flattax98>{{cite news|last=Pflieger|first=Martin|title=Afflerbach, Toomey Disagree on Flat Tax|url=http://pqasb.pqarchiver.com/mcall/access/34538946.html?dids=34538946:34538946&FMT=ABS&FMTS=ABS:FT&type=current&date=Sep+24%2C+1998&author=MARTIN+PFLIEGER%2C+The+Morning+Call&pub=Morning+Call&desc=AFFLERBACH%2C+TOOMEY+DISAGREE+ON+FLAT+TAX+*+SENATOR%3A+IT+WOULD+CREATE+HUGE+DEFICIT%2C+BUST+SURPLUS.&pqatl=google|accessdate=16 August 2011|newspaper=The Morning Call|date=24 September 1998}}</ref>  . 

Toomey won by a ten-point margin. Toomey was reelected two more times by relatively comfortable margins. He did not run for reelection to his House seat in 2004, fulfilling a pledge that he had signed in 1998 to serve only three terms. <ref name=houseelect/> . 

===Tenure===. 
Toomey served as the [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] for {{ushr|PA|15}} from 1999 to 2005. While serving in the United States House of Representatives he distinguished himself as a fiscal expert. Honoring his three-term pledge, he did not seek reelection in 2004. <ref>[http://toomey.senate.gov/about.cfm Toomey.senate.gov]</ref> . 

==U.S. Senate==. 
===Elections===. 
[[File:Pat Toomey by Gage Skidmore.jpg|right|thumb|Toomey speaking at CPAC in February 2011.]] . 

In [[United States Senate election in Pennsylvania, 2004|2004]], Toomey challenged [[incumbent]] [[United States Senate|Senator]] [[Arlen Specter]] in the Republican [[primary election]]. Aided by $2 million of advertising from the [[Club for Growth]], Toomey's [[election campaign]] theme was that Specter was not a conservative, especially on fiscal issues. However, most of the state's Republican establishment closed ranks behind Specter. This included endorsements by Pennsylvania's other Senator, [[Rick Santorum]] and by President [[George W. Bush]]. Toomey lost the election by a 1.7% margin.  . 

On April 15, 2009, Toomey announced his intention to once again challenge Specter in the [[United States Senate election in Pennsylvania, 2010|Republican senatorial primary]]. <ref name="Senate 2010">{{cite news|url=http://www.foxnews.com/politics/2009/04/15/specter-faces-conservative-challenge-familiar-foe|title=Specter Faces Conservative Challenge From Familiar Foe|publisher=[[Fox News]] |first=Trish|last=Turner|date=April 15, 2009}}</ref> On April 28, 2009, Specter announced that he was switching parties and would run as a Democrat in 2010 after polls showed him losing to Toomey in the primary. <ref>{{cite news|last=|first=|coauthors=Ed Hornick and Deirdre Walsh|title=Longtime GOP Sen. Arlen Specter becomes Democrat|work=|location=|pages=|publisher=CNN|date=28 April 2009|url=http://www.cnn.com/2009/POLITICS/04/28/specter.party.switch/index.html|accessdate=}}</ref> Specter's withdrawal left Toomey as the front runner for the 2010 Republican nomination. <ref>{{cite news|last=Dale|first=Maryclaire|coauthors=|title=Rep. Sestak will try to unseat Sen. Specter of Pa.|work=|location=|pages=|agency = Associated Press|date=4 August 2009|url=http://www.google.com/hostednews/ap/article/ALeqM5hRLeivXVE_840fD7Tlr0coHPlE1QD99S8ROG0|accessdate=}}</ref> Toomey won the Republican Senate primary on May 18, 2010.  On November 2, 2010 he was elected to the United States Senate defeating Democrat [[Joe Sestak]]. <ref name="Httpwwwwashingtontimescomnewsnovtoomeywinsspectersseatpennsylvania">[http://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2010/nov/3/toomey-wins-specters-seat-pennsylvania/ Washingtontimes.com]</ref> . 

===Tenure===. 
Toomey was elected to the United States Senate on November 2, 2010. His term began on January 3, 2011. He joined the [[Congressional Hispanic Conference]], a caucus which he was an original member of in his days in the House.  . 

On August 11, 2011, Toomey was named to the [[United States Congress Joint Select Committee on Deficit Reduction]] by Senate minority leader [[Mitch McConnell]]. The committee's duties included composing a package of spending cuts for submission to both Houses of Congress. <ref name=debtcomm>{{cite news|title=Toomey Named to Debt Super Committee|url=http://www.politicspa.com/breaking-toomey-named-to-debt-super-committee/26764/|accessdate=15 August 2011|newspaper=Politics PA}}</ref> . 

Toomey and the rest of the committee failed to do this. <ref name="statement20111121">{{cite web| title=Statement from Co-Chairs of the Joint Select Committee on Deficit Reduction | url= http://www.deficitreduction.gov/public/index.cfm/2011/11/statement-from-co-chairs-of-the-joint-select-committee-on-deficit-reduction |date=November 21, 2011| accessdate= November 21, 2011| publisher=deficitreduction.gov}}</ref> . 

Toomey is the first [[Lehigh Valley]] resident to serve as United States Senator from Pennsylvania since [[Richard Brodhead]], in the mid-19th century. <ref name=brodhead>{{cite web|title=Toomey Triumphs|url=http://www.toomeyforsenate.com/content/toomey-triumphs|accessdate=15 August 2011}}</ref> .

==Political positions==. 
[[File:Pat Toomey - Philadelphia Tea Party II 2009.jpg|right|thumb|300px|U.S. Senate candidate Pat Toomey addresses the [[Philadelphia]] [[Tea Party protests|Tea Party]] on April 18, 2009.]]
Based on his three terms in United States House of Representatives, the conservative [[American Conservative Union]] (ACU) has assigned Toomey a lifetime congressional rating of 97%,<ref name="Human Events">{{cite news|last=Murdock|first=Deroy|title=Pat Toomey May Take Arlen Specter Out of GOP's Misery|publisher=Human Events|date=23 March 2009|url=http://findarticles.com/p/articles/mi_qa3827/is_20090323/ai_n31534929|accessdate=3 May 2009}}</ref> and the liberal [[Americans for Democratic Action]] (ADA) a lifetime "Liberal Quotient" of 6%. <ref name="ADA Today Newsletter">{{cite journal|title=Voting Records|journal=ADA Today Newsletter|volume=55-60|issue=1|publisher=Americans for Democratic Action|date=2000-2006|url=http://www.adaction.org/pages/publications/voting-records.php|accessdate=}}</ref> The [[ACLU]] has given Toomey a 13% lifetime rating on civil rights. <ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ontheissues.org/PA/Pat_Toomey_Civil_Rights.htm|title=ACLU rating for Toomey|publisher=Ontheissues.org|date=|accessdate=2010-06-13}}</ref> . 

In January 2005 Toomey became president of the [[Club for Growth]], which had greatly supported his 2004 race against Specter.  . 

===Abortion===. 
Toomey identifies as [[pro-life]]. Toomey received a 100% by the [[National Right to Life Committee]]. He has stated that he supports imposing jail terms for doctors who perform abortions. <ref>{{Cite episode | title = Hardball with Chris Matthews | episodelink = | url = http://www.msnbc.msn.com/id/32284387/ns/msnbc_tv-hardball_with_chris_matthews/t/hardball-chris-matthews-tuesday-august/| network = [[msnbc]] | airdate = 4 Aug 2009| minutes = | transcript = "MATTHEWS: Would you put people in jail for performing abortions? TOOMEY: At some point, doctors performing abortions, I think, would - would be subject to that sort of penalty."| transcripturl= http://www.msnbc.msn.com/id/32284387/ns/msnbc_tv-hardball_with_chris_matthews/t/hardball-chris-matthews-tuesday-august/}}</ref> . 

===Earmarks===. 
In his first term as a congressman, Toomey won $9 million overall in earmark funding to his district. <ref>[http://www.philly.com/inquirer/local/pa/20100908_Toomey_got_millions_in_earmarks_before_railing_against_them.html#ixzz0yzzRyMVF "Toomey got millions in earmarks before railing against them"], Thomas Fitzgerald. [[Philadelphia Inquirer]]. Wednesday, September 8. 2010. Accessed September 8, 2010. </ref> In successive terms in Congress, he swore off earmarks and signed the "No Pork" pledge as a senate candidate.{{Citation needed|date=October 2010}} . 

===Education===. 
Toomey has strongly supported increased [[school choice]] and [[charter school]]s. <ref>[http://www.toomeyforsenate.com/node/48]</ref> . 

===Environment===. 
During Toomey's tenure in Congress, he supported legislation that would speed up approval of forest thinning projects in 2003, supported opening the [[Arctic National Wildlife Refuge]] to oil drilling and development, opposed implementing the [[Kyoto Protocol]], and opposed legislation that would mandate increased vehicle fuel efficiency standards and provide incentives for [[alternative energy|alternative fuels]]. <ref name="On the issues"/> In 2003, Toomey was given a 0% rating by the [[League of Conservation Voters]], indicating what the organization considers to be an anti-environmental voting record.  . 

===Financial regulation===. 
Since his first days as a freshman Congressman, Toomey has strongly advocated deregulation of the financial services industry: "The trend in deregulation, beginning in the early 1980s, is one of the biggest reasons for the sustained economic expansion. I would like to see us continue to deregulate on many fronts, including the financial services industry,” he stated in the spring of 1999. <ref name=ds/> . 

While serving on the [[United States House Committee on Financial Services|House Banking Committee]], Toomey, in 1999, helped write House Resolution 10, which led to the repeal of parts of the Depression-era [[Glass-Steagall Act]]. <ref name=ds/><ref name="Time has come"/> The repeal of the Act, which had regulated the separation of banks and investment firms, allowed for companies that combined banking and investment operations. President [[Barack Obama]] and [[Elizabeth Warren]], former Chair of the [[Oversight of the Troubled Asset Relief Program#Congressional Oversight Panel .28COP.29|Congressional Oversight Panel on TARP]] have said that the repeal of Glass-Steagall was a significant cause of the current [[Global financial crisis of 2008–2009|global financial crisis]], on the grounds that it opened up the door for the financial sector to form so-called ''[[too big to fail]]'' financial services giants like [[AIG]]. <ref name="Time has come"/><ref name=dailyshow>{{cite episode | title = Elizabeth Warren | url = http://www.thedailyshow.com/watch/tue-january-26-2010/elizabeth-warren | series = The Daily Show with Jon Stewart | network = Comedy Central | airdate = Tuesday January 26, 2010 | minutes = 0:45 | transcripturl= http://lincmad.blogspot.com/2009/04/tarp-cop-on-daily-show.html}}</ref> . 

===Guns===. 
Toomey advocates reducing gun regulations. While serving in Congress, Toomey supported bills that would prohibit suing gun makers and sellers for gun misuse and would decrease the waiting period due to [[background check]]s from three days to one for purchases made at [[gun show]]s. <ref name="On the issues"/> Toomey received an "A" by the [[National Rifle Association]] (NRA), indicating what the NRA considers to be a pro-gun rights voting record.  . 

===LGBT issues===. 
Toomey has supported several legislative actions designed to block the expansion of certain civil rights to homosexuals, or to revoke existing rights; specifically, he voted to amend the [[U.S. Constitution]] to ban [[same-sex marriage]];<ref name="On the issues"/> to ban gay adoptions in the District of Columbia; and for the [[Marriage Protection Act]] of 2004, a bill that would amend the [[federal judicial code]] to deny [[United States federal courts|federal courts]] jurisdiction to hear or decide any question pertaining to the interpretation of the [[Defense of Marriage Act]] or of the Marriage Protection Act itself. <ref name=fl>{{cite web|url=http://writ.news.findlaw.com/grossman/20040727.html|title=The Proposed Marriage Protection Act: Why It May Be Unconstitutional|author=Grossman, Joanna|date=2004-07-27|accessdate=2008-02-15|publisher=[[FindLaw]]|work=Writ}}</ref> . 

Toomey did, however, support the repeal of [[Don't Ask, Don't Tell]], the policy which bans openly gay or bisexual persons from serving in the military, in a statement made while he was Senator-elect. <ref>[http://www.politicspa.com/breaking-toomey-supports-repeal-of-dont-ask-dont-tell/19361/ Politicspa.com]</ref> . 

===Healthcare===. 
Toomey opposed the passage of the [[Medicare Prescription Drug, Improvement, and Modernization Act|Medicare Prescription Drug Act]] which he argued was fiscally irresponsible. <ref>{{cite web|url=http://clerk.house.gov/evs/2003/roll669.xml|title=Final Vote Results for Roll Call 669|publisher=clerk.house.gov|date=|accessdate=2010-09-04}}</ref> . 

Toomey's 2012 budget proposal called for turning Medicaid into a block grant to states and cutting federal funding for the program by half by 2021. <ref name=CBPP>{{cite web|last=Horney|first=James R.|title=Toomey Budget Even More Radical, and Potentially More Damaging, Than Ryan Budget|url=http://www.cbpp.org/cms/index.cfm?fa=view&id=3504|publisher=Center on Budget and Policy Priorities|accessdate=21 June 2011}}</ref> . 

=== Stop Online Piracy Act (SOPA) / Protect Intellectual Property Act (PIPA) ===. 
Toomey has taken a stance against both [[SOPA]] and [[PROTECT IP Act|PIPA]] citing "flaws" in the Bills and has vowed to work together with other Senators to establish a more appropriate way of combating online piracy in the United States.  . 

===Taxes and government spending===. 
Toomey has been a consistent advocate of reducing and eliminating taxes. While in Congress he voted to reduce the capital gains tax, to eliminate the [[Estate tax in the United States|estate tax]], to cut small business taxes, to eliminate the "[[marriage penalty]]", to first cut federal income taxes and other taxes by $958B over 10 years (the [[Economic Growth and Tax Relief Reconciliation Act of 2001]]) and later to make these cuts permanent, to reduce capital gains and income taxes by nearly $100 billion (the Economic Security and Recovery Act of 2002), and to expand and extend multiple tax credits to individuals and businesses. <ref name="On the issues">{{cite web|title=Pat Toomey on the Issues|publisher=OnTheIssues.org|date=January 2005|url=http://www.ontheissues.org/PA/Pat_Toomey.htm|accessdate=2 May 2009}}</ref> . 

Toomey publicly opposed the 2009 federal stimulus package. He opposes government-run or subsidized healthcare. Toomey opposed farm subsidies and called farm subsidies in a 2009 article in U.S.A Today "Moscow on the Mississippi" He has supported a federal balanced budget amendment. {{Citation needed|date=September 2010}} . 

Toomey was rated 80% by the [[National Taxpayers Union]] (NTU), classifying him as what the NTU considers to be a "Taxpayer's Friend" on tax votes. <ref name="On the issues"/> From 1999-2004, He received a 78% rating by the [[Republican Liberty Caucus]] (RLC) on its 'Liberty Index', indicating what the RLC considers his career to be, on average, one of a libertarian-leaning voting record. However, it should be noted that throughout his career, his Liberty Index rating has always decreased, starting at 89% in for 1999–2000, 84.5% in 2001, 79.5% for 2002–2003, 75% in 2004, and 65% in 2005; suggesting that Toomey is increasingly a [[Centrism|centrist]]. <ref name="RLC Index Lookup">{{cite web|title=Pat Toomey rating|publisher=RepublicanLiberty.org|date=January 2005|url=http://www.republicanliberty.org/libdex/li_look.asp|accessdate=2 Nov 2010}}</ref> In 2003, Toomey was given a 90% rating by the [[United States Chamber of Commerce]].  .

==Personal life==. 
In November 1997, Toomey married Kris Ann Duncan, a childhood friend of Toomey's sister. Duncan is a full-time homemaker. The Toomey's have three children: Bridget, Patrick and Duncan.  .

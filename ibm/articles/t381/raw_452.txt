A [[monarch]] is the person who [[Head of state|heads]] a [[monarchy]], a form of government in which a state or polity is ruled by an individual who normally rules for [[Life tenure|life]] or until [[abdication]], and typically [[inheritance|inherits]] the throne by birth. <ref>{{Cite web
  | author = American Heritage Dictionary of the English Language
  | coauthors = Collins English Dictionary: Complete and Unabridged
  | title = Monarch
  | work = The Free Dictionary
  | publisher = Farlex, Inc
  | url = http://www.thefreedictionary.com/monarch
  | accessdate = 10 August 2010}}</ref> Monarchs may be [[Autocracy|autocrats]] (as in many [[absolute monarchy|absolute monarch]]ies)<ref>{{Cite book
  | last = Herb
  | first = Michael
  | title = All in the family: absolutism, revolution, and democracy in the Middle East
  | publisher = SUNY Press
  | year = 1999
  | location = New York
  | page = 235
  | url = http://books.google.com/books?id=i6hd9w64lcgC
  | isbn = 9780791441688}}</ref> or may be [[Traditional authority|ceremonial]] figureheads who exercise only [[reserve power]], with actual authority vested in a [[parliament]] or other governing bodies (as in many [[constitutional monarchy|constitutional monarch]]ies). <ref>{{Cite book
  | last = Spellman
  | first = W. M. 
  | title = Monarchies 1000–2000
  | publisher = Reaktion Books
  | year = 2001
  | location = London
  | pages = 22–23
  | url = http://books.google.com/books?id=MRbExiEuYPsC| isbn = 9781861890870}}</ref> In many cases, a monarch will also be [[Divine right of kings|linked]] with a state religion. <ref>{{Cite book
  | last = Harris
  | first = Nathanial
  | title = Systems of Government: Monarchy
  | publisher = Evans Brothers
  | year = 2009
  | location = London
  | page = 38
  | url = http://books.google.com.my/books?id=o5SKJanyblIC| isbn = 9780237539320}}</ref> Most states only have a single monarch at any given time, although a [[regent]] may rule when the monarch is a [[Minor (law)|minor]], [[Absenteeism|not present]], or [[Capacity (law)|otherwise incapable]] of ruling. <ref>{{cite web
  | author = American Heritage Dictionary of the English Language
  | coauthors = Collins English Dictionary: Complete and Unabridged
  | title = Regent
  | work = The Free Dictionary
  | publisher = Farlex, Inc
  | url = http://www.thefreedictionary.com/regent
  | accessdate = 10 August 2010}}</ref> Cases in which two monarchs rule simultaneously over a single state, as is the current situation in Andorra, are known as [[coregency|coregencies]]. <ref name="hindley">{{Cite book

Monarchs are distinguished by their [[Royal and noble ranks|titles]] and [[Style (manner of address)|styles]], which in most cases are defined by tradition, and guaranteed under the state's [[constitution]]. A variety of titles are applied in English; for example, "king" and "queen", "prince" and "princess", "emperor" and "empress". Although they will be addressed differently in their local languages, the [[Regnal name|names]] and titles in the list below have been styled using the common English equivalent. [[Roman numerals]], used to distinguish related rulers with the same name,<ref>{{Cite book
  | title = Merriam-Webster's manual for writers and editors
  | publisher = Merriam-Webster, Inc
  | year = 1998
  | location = Springfield, United States
  | page = 94
  | url = http://books.google.com/books?id=7C6V9zRxSPkC
  | isbn = 9780877796220}}</ref> have been applied where typical.  . 

In political and sociocultural studies, monarchies are normally associated with [[Hereditary monarchy|hereditary rule]]; most monarchs, in both historical and contemporary contexts, have been born and raised within a [[royal family]]. <ref name="hindley"/><ref>{{Cite book
  | last = Forty
  | first = Sandra
  | coauthors = Millidge, Judith; Riley, Ed
  | title = World Royal Families
  | publisher = Book Sales, Inc
  | year = 2009
  | location = United States
  | page = 94
  | isbn = 9780785825302}}</ref> [[Order of succession|Succession]] has been defined using a variety of distinct formulae, such as [[proximity of blood]], [[primogeniture]], and [[agnatic seniority]]. Some monarchies, however, are not hereditary, and the ruler is instead determined through an [[Elective monarchy|elective]] process; a modern example is the throne of Malaysia. <ref>[[s:Constitution of Malaysia#Article 32|Constitution of Malaysia, Art. 32, Sec. 3]]. </ref> These systems defy the model concept of a monarchy, but are commonly considered as such because they retain certain associative characteristics. <ref>{{Cite book
  | last = Bouvier
  | first = John
  | coauthors = Rawle, Francis
  | title = Bouvier's Law Dictionary and Concise Encyclopedia
  | volume = 2
  | edition = 3rd
  | publisher = Vernon Law Book Company
  | year = 1914
  | pages = 2237–2238}}</ref> Many systems use a combination of hereditary and elective elements, where the election or nomination of a successor is restricted to members of a [[Royal descent|royal bloodline]]. <ref>{{Cite book
  | last = Shawcross
  | first = William
  | title = Cambodia's new deal: a report
  | publisher = Carnegie Endowment for International Peace
  | year = 1994
  | page = 106
  | isbn = 9780870030512}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book

  | isbn = 9781115595995}}</ref> .

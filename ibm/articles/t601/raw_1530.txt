As of April 2010, the [[unemployment]] rate in the [[United States]] was 9.9%, but the government’s broader U-6 unemployment rate was 17.1%. <ref>"[http://blogs.wsj.com/economics/2010/05/07/broader-u-6-unemployment-rate-increases-to-171-in-april/ Broader U-6 Unemployment Rate Increases to 17.1% in April]". [[The Wall Street Journal]]. May 7, 2010. </ref> There are six unemployed people, on average, for each available job. <ref name="unemployment"/> Men account for at least 7 of 10 workers who lost jobs, according to Bureau of Labor Statistics data. <ref>"[http://articles.latimes.com/2010/mar/22/opinion/la-oe-kuhn22-2010mar22?pg=2 Revenge of the white men]". Los Angeles Times. March 22, 2010 (Page 2 of 2). </ref> The youth unemployment rate was 18.5% in July 2009, the highest July rate since 1948. <ref>"[http://www.bls.gov/news.release/youth.nr0.htm Employment and Unemployment Among Youth Summary]". United States Department of Labor. </ref> 34.5% of young African American men were unemployed in October 2009. <ref>"[http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/article/2009/11/23/AR2009112304092.html Blacks hit hard by economy's punch]". The Washington Post. November 24, 2009. </ref> Officially, [[Detroit]]’s unemployment rate is 27%, but the ''Detroit News'' suggests that nearly half of this city’s working-age population may be unemployed. <ref>"[http://detnews.com/article/20091216/METRO01/912160374/Nearly-half-of-Detroit-s-workers-are-unemployed Nearly half of Detroit's workers are unemployed]". The Detroit News. December 16, 2009. </ref>  3.8 million Americans lost their jobs in 2009. <ref name="unemployment">"[http://www.msnbc.msn.com/id/34641146/ns/business-stocks_and_economy/ Unemployment sets a grim record in 2009]". Msnbc.msn.com. December 31, 2009. </ref> . 

In ''Out of Work: Unemployment and Government in the Twentieth-Century America'', economists [[Richard Vedder]] and Lowell Gallaway argue that the empirical record of wages rates, productivity, and unemployment in American validates the classical unemployment theory. Their data shows a strong correlation between the adjusted real wage and unemployment in the [[United States]] from 1900 to 1990. However, they maintain that their data take into account [[exogeny|exogenous events]].  .

==Unemployment in popular culture==. 
John Steinbeck's 1939 novel ''[[Grapes of Wrath]]'' is set during the [[Great Depression]] and focuses on the Joads, a poor family of sharecroppers driven from their Oklahoma home by drought, economic hardship, and changes in financial and agricultural industries.  .
